The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

First form

Theorem: Let $F \colon [a,b] \to \R$ be a continuous function, differentiable on $(a,b)$. Let $f \in \mathcal R[a,b]$ be such that $f(x) = F'(x)$ for $x \in (a,b)$. Then \begin{equation*} \int_a^b f = F(b)-F(a) . \end{equation*}

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

Second form

Theorem: Let $f \colon [a,b] \to \R$ be a Riemann integrable function. Define \begin{equation*} F(x) := \int_a^x f . \end{equation*} First, $F$ is continuous on $[a,b]$. Second, if $f$ is continuous at $c \in [a,b]$, then $F$ is differentiable at $c$ and $F'(c) = f(c)$.

Change of variables

Theorem: Let $g \colon [a,b] \to \R$ be a continuously differentiable function, let $f \colon [c,d] \to \R$ be continuous, and suppose $g\bigl([a,b]\bigr) \subset [c,d]$. Then \begin{equation*} \int_a^b f\bigl(g(x)\bigr)\, g'(x)~ dx = \int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(s)~ ds . \end{equation*}

The logarithm

Theorem: There exists a unique function $L \colon (0,\infty) \to \R$ such that

  1. $L(1) = 0$.
  2. $L$ is differentiable and $L'(x) = \frac{1}{x}$.
  3. $L$ is strictly increasing, bijective, and \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 0} L(x) = -\infty , \qquad \text{and} \qquad \lim_{x\to \infty} L(x) = \infty . \end{equation*}
  4. $L(xy) = L(x)+L(y)$ for all $x,y \in (0,\infty)$.
  5. If $q$ is a rational number and $x > 0$, then $L(x^q) = q L(x)$.

The exponential

Theorem: There exists a unique function $E \colon \R \to (0,\infty)$ such that

  1. $E(0) = 1$.
  2. $E$ is differentiable and $E'(x) = E(x)$.
  3. $E$ is strictly increasing, bijective, and \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to -\infty} E(x) = 0 , \qquad \text{and} \qquad \lim_{x\to \infty} E(x) = \infty . \end{equation*}
  4. $E(x+y) = E(x)E(y)$ for all $x,y \in \R$.
  5. If $q$ is a rational number, then $E(qx) = E(x)^q$.

The exponential

Define the exponential function as \begin{equation*} \exp(x) := E(x) . \end{equation*}

If $y \in \Q$ and $x > 0$, then \begin{equation*} x^y = \exp\bigl(\ln(x^y)\bigr) = \exp\bigl(y\ln(x)\bigr) . \end{equation*}

We can now make sense of exponentiation $x^y$ for arbitrary $y \in \R$; if $x > 0$ and $y$ is irrational, define \begin{equation*} x^y := \exp\bigl(y\ln(x)\bigr) . \end{equation*}

The exponential

Define the number $e$, sometimes called Euler's number or the base of the natural logarithm, as \begin{equation*} e := \exp(1) . \end{equation*}

The notation $e^x$ for $\exp(x)$: \begin{equation*} e^x = \exp\bigl(x \ln(e) \bigr) = \exp(x) . \end{equation*}
