Derivative of \(\log z\)

\(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dz}\log z\):   Consider \(|z|>0\) and recall

\[ \begin{align} \log z & = \ln |z| + i \,\arg(z) \\ & = \ln r + i \theta. \end{align} \]

\( \implies u = \ln r, v = \theta\)

\( \implies u_r = \displaystyle \frac{1}{r}, u_{\theta} = 0 , v_r =0, v_{\theta} = 1.\)

Derivative of \(\log z\)

C/R in polar coordinates: \[ \begin{align} ru_r & = v_{\theta} \\ u_{\theta} & = -ru_r \end{align} \]

Satisfies sufficient conditions for complex differentiability on any subset of \(\C_*\), such that \(\alpha < \theta < \alpha + 2 \pi\), with \(\alpha\) fixed in \(\R\).

Derivative of \(\log z\)

So if we specify a branch, we have that \(\log\) is differentiable and Lecture 15 \( \implies \)

\( \displaystyle \frac{d}{dz}\log z \) \( = e^{-i\theta} \left( u_r + i v_r \right)\) \( = e^{-i\theta}\displaystyle \frac{1}{r}\) \( = \displaystyle \frac{1}{re^{i\theta}}\) \( = \displaystyle \frac{1}{z}\)

Derivative of \(\log z\)

E. g. \( \displaystyle \frac{d}{dz}\Log\, z \) \( \displaystyle = \frac{1}{z} \)  for \( -\pi < \Arg \, z< \pi \), \(|z|> 0\).

Derivative of \(z^c\)

For \(f(z)= z^c\), with \(c\in \C_*\) fixed, defined on \(\C_*\):

\[f(z) = \exp\left( c \log z \right) \]

\(f'(z) = \exp\left( c \log z \right) \cdot \frac{c}{z} \)     \((*)\)

\(\qquad = z^c \cdot \frac{c}{z} \) \( = c z^{c-1} \)      \((**)\)

Derivative of \(z^c\)

\((**)\) is valid on aby domain of the form \[\left\{ z: |z|>0, \alpha <\arg\, z < \alpha + 2\pi\right\}\]

(due to needing to pick a branch of \(\log z\) in \((*)\)).

Notation from Real Analysis

\(\Omega \subseteq \R^n\)

\(*\) \(C\left( \Omega \right)\) \( = C^0\left( \Omega \right)\)

\(\quad\qquad = \left\{ \text{continuous functions}: \Omega \ra \R \right\}\)

\(*\) \( C^k\left( \Omega \right)\) \( = \left\{ \text{functions } f:\Omega \ra \R:\right. \) \(\qquad \quad\left. f \text{ and all its derivatives/partial derivatives } \right.\) \(\qquad \quad\left. \text{of order } 0,1,\ldots, k \text{ exist and are continuos} \right\}\)

Note: \(0\)th order derivative of \(f\) is \(f\).

Notation from Real Analysis

\(*\) \( C^{\infty}\left( \Omega \right)\) \( = \left\{ \text{functions } f:\Omega \ra \R:\right. \) \(\qquad \quad\left. f \text{ and all its derivatives/partial derivatives } \right.\) \(\qquad \quad\left. \text{of all orders exist and are continuos} \right\}\)

Notation from Real Analysis

\(*\) \( f\in C^{\omega} \left( \Omega \right)\) says at every \(x_0\in \Omega\):

  1. \(f\) has a power series expansion about \(x_0\). Namely, its Taylor series.
  2. \(f\) is given by its power series expansion, i.e. power series converges to \(f\) on some neighbourhood of \(x_0\). a.k.a real analytic functions.

Note: (i) \(\Rightarrow f\in C^{\infty}(\Omega)\) ,    (i) \(\nRightarrow \) (ii) \(\in \R^{n}\).

Notation from Real Analysis

Consider \[ f(x) = \left\{ \begin{array}{lr} e^{-1/x^2} & x > 0\\ 0 & x \le 0 \end{array} \right. \]

Check sufficient conditions for \(f'\) to exist:

a) \(f= u+iv \) \( = e^{x+iy} \) \( = e^x \left(\cos y +i \sin y\right) \) defined on \(\C\).

\( u = e^x\cos y, v = e^{x}\sin y \).   \(u_x, u_y, v_x, v_y \) defined and continuous on \(\C\).

b) Check C/R: \(u_x=v_y\) & \(u_y = -v_x \)   or \( \displaystyle \frac{\partial f}{\partial \overline{z}}=0. \)

Wirtinger operators

Application 2

When is \(g(z) = |z|^2\) differentiable?

Note: \(g(z)=z\overline{z} = x^2+y^2\).

C/R: \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial g}{\partial \overline{z}}= 0 \) \( \implies z = 0 \)

So \(g\) cannot be differentiable for \(z\neq 0\) because C/R is necessary for differentiability.

Wirtinger operators

Application 2

At \(z=0\) check sufficient conditions:

\(u,v\) are defined on \(\C\). \(u_x,u_y,v_x,v_y\) are defined and continuous on \(\C\) (a neighbourhood of \(z=0\)).

C/R hold. So \(g'(0)=0\).

Analytic and singular function

Definition: \(f:\Omega \ra \C\) is analytic at \(z_0\) if \(f\) is differentiable on a neighbourhood of \(z_0\).

A function is singular at \(z_0\) if it is NOT analytic at \(z_0\), but is analytic at some point in every neighbouhood of \(z_0\).

Analytic and singular function

E. g.   \(f(z)=\displaystyle \frac{1}{z}\) is analytic on \(\C_*\) and singular at \(0\) on \(B_{\epsilon}(0)\),   \(f\) is analytic on \(B_{\epsilon'}(z_0)\), \(z_0\in B_{\epsilon}(0)\), \(\epsilon'<|z_0|\).

Entire function

A function is entire if it is analytic on all of \(\C\).

E. g. Polynomials, \(\sin,\cos, \exp, \cosh, \sinh\).

Note: If a function is differentiable at precisely on a point, it is not analytic there, or anywhere. E. g. \(|z|^2\).
