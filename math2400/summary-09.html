Sequences of functions

Pointwise convergence

Definition: For every $n \in \N$, let $f_n \colon S \to \R$ be a function. The sequence $\{ f_n \}_{n=1}^\infty$ converges pointwise to $f \colon S \to \R$ if for every $x \in S$, we have \begin{equation*} f(x) = \lim_{n\to\infty} f_n(x) . \end{equation*}

Sequences of functions

Pointwise convergence

Example: Define $f_n$ on $[-1,1]$ as \[ \displaystyle f_n(x):=x^{2n}. \] $f_n$ converges to $f:[-1,1]\ra \R$, where \[ f(x)=\left\{\begin{array}{ll} 1 & \text{if }x=-1,1 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{array} \right. \]

Sequences of functions

Pointwise convergence

Theorem: Let $f_n \colon S \to \R$ and $f \colon S \to \R$ be functions. Then $\{ f_n \}$ converges pointwise to $f$ if and only if for every $x \in S$, and every $\epsilon \gt 0$, there exists an $N \in \N$ such that for all $n \geq N$, we have \begin{equation*} \abs{\,f_n(x)-f(x)} \lt \epsilon . \end{equation*}

Sequences of functions

Uniform convergence

Theorem: Let $f_n \colon S \to \R$ and $f \colon S \to \R$ be functions. The sequence $\{ f_n \}$ converges uniformly to $f$ if for every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists an $N \in \N$ such that for all $n \geq N$, we have \begin{equation*} \abs{\,f_n(x) - f(x)} \lt \epsilon \quad \text{for all } x \in S. \end{equation*}

Uniform convergence

Theorem: Let $\{ f_n \}$ be a sequence of functions $f_n \colon S \to \R$. If $\{ f_n \}$ converges uniformly to $f \colon S \to \R$, then converges pointwise to $f$.

Sequences of functions

Nonuniform convergence criterion

Theorem: Let $f_n \colon S \to \R$ and $f \colon S \to \R$ be functions.

The sequence $\{ f_n \}$ does not converge uniformly

if and only if

for some $\epsilon_0\gt 0$ there is a subsequence $\{ f_{n_k} \}$ of $\{ f_n \}$ and a sequence $\{ x_k \}$ in $S$ such that \[ \abs{\,f_{n_k}\left(x_k\right)-f(x_k)}\geq \epsilon_0 \text{ for all } k\in \N. \]

Sequences of functions

Nonuniform convergence criterion

Example: Let $f_n(x) := \dfrac{x}{n}$ defined on $\R$ for every $n\in \N$. Observe that $\{f_n\}$ converges to $f=0$, since \[ \lim f_n(x) = \lim \frac{x}{n} = x\lim\frac{1}{n}=0 \;\text{ for all }\,x\in \R. \]

If we let $n_k:=k$ and $x_k := k $. Then $f_{n_k}(x_k)=1$ so that \[ \abs{\,f_{n_k}(x_k)-f(x_k)}= \abs{1-0}=1. \] Therefore the sequence $\{f_n\}$ does not converges uniformly to $f$.

Sequences of functions

Convergence in uniform norm

Definition: Let $f \colon S \to \R$ be a bounded function. Define \begin{equation*} \norm{\,f}_u := \sup \bigl\{ \abs{\,f(x)} : x \in S \bigr\} . \end{equation*} We call $\norm{\cdot}_u$ the uniform norm.

Sequences of functions

Convergence in uniform norm

Theorem: A sequence of bounded functions $f_n \colon S \to \R$ converges uniformly to $f \colon S \to \R$, if and only if \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to\infty} \norm{\,f_n - f}_u = 0 . \end{equation*}

Theorem: Let $f_n \colon S \to \R$ be bounded functions. Then $\{ f_n \}$ is Cauchy in the uniform norm if and only if there exists an $f \colon S \to \R$ and $\{ f_n \}$ converges uniformly to $f$.

Sequences of functions

Interchange of limits

Theorem: Let $\{ f_n \}$ be a sequence of continuous functions $f_n \colon S \to \R$ converging uniformly to $f \colon S \to \R$. Then $f$ is continuous.

Sequences of functions

Integral of the limit

Theorem: Let $\{ f_n \}$ be a sequence of Riemann integrable functions $f_n \colon [a,b] \to \R$ converging uniformly to $f \colon [a,b] \to \R$. Then $f$ is Riemann integrable, and \begin{equation*} \int_a^b f = \lim_{n\to\infty} \int_a^b f_n . \end{equation*}

Sequences of functions

Derivative of the limit

Theorem: Let $I$ be a bounded interval and let $f_n \colon I \to \R$ be continuously differentiable functions. Suppose $\{ f_n' \}$ converges uniformly to $g \colon I \to \R$, and suppose $\bigl\{ f_n(c) \bigr\}_{n=1}^\infty$ is a convergent sequence for some $c \in I$. Then $\{ f_n \}$ converges uniformly to a continuously differentiable function $f \colon I \to \R$, and $f' = g$.

Fourier series

The Fourier series of a real function $f$ defined on the interval $(-p,p)$ is given by \[ f(x)=\frac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left(a_n\,\cos \left(\frac{n\, \pi}{p}x\right)+b_n\,\sin \left(\frac{n\, \pi}{p}x\right) \right), \] where \begin{eqnarray*} a_0&=&\frac{1}{p}\int_{-p}^pf(x)\,dx,\\ a_n&=&\frac{1}{p}\int_{-p}^pf(x)\,\cos \left(\frac{n\, \pi}{p}x\right)\,dx,\\ b_n&=&\frac{1}{p}\int_{-p}^pf(x)\,\sin \left(\frac{n\, \pi}{p}x\right)\,dx. \end{eqnarray*}

The Fourier series is named in honor of Jean Baptiste Joseph Fourier (1768-1830).



Fourier series

Example: Consider the function defined by $f(x)=x+\pi$ for $-\pi < x < \pi $ and $f(x)=f(x+2\pi)$ for $-\infty < x < \infty$. Its Fourier series expansion is \[ f(x)=\pi+2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{\left(-1\right)^{n+1}}{n}\sin (n\,x). \]
