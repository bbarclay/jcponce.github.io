13.1 Geometric interpretation of vector functions

We say that $f$ maps $x$ to $p$, and that $p$ is the image of $x$ under $f$. If $S\subset D_f$, where $D_f$ is the domain of $f$, then the set \[ f(S) := \left\{ u : u = f(x), \,x\in S\right\} \] is the image of $S$ under $f$.

Remark: We will often denote the components of elements in $S$ by $x, y, z\ldots,$ and the components of elements in $f(S)$ by $u, v, w\ldots.$

$u = f_1(x,y) = x^2+y^2\;$ and $\;v = f_2(x,y) = x^2-y^2.$

13.2 Invertible functions

A transformation $f$ is one-to-one, or invertible, if $f(x)$ and $f(y)$ are distinct whenever $x$ and $y$ are distinct points of $D_f$.

In this case, we can define a function $g$ on the range \[ R(f) := \left\{ u: u = f(x), \text{ for some } x\in D_f \right\} \] of $f$ by defining $g(u)$ to be the unique point in $D_f$ such that $f(u)=u.$

Then $\, D_g = R(f)\;$ and $\;R(g) = D_f.$


13.2 Invertible functions

In this case, we can define a function $g$ on the range \[ R(f) := \left\{ u: u = f(x), \text{ for some } x\in D_f \right\} \] of $f$ by defining $g(u)$ to be the unique point in $D_f$ such that $f(u)=u.$

Then $\, D_g = R(f)\;$ and $\;R(g) = D_f.$

Moreover, $g$ is one-to-one. So we have

$g\left(f(x)\right)= x,\; x \in D_f\; $ and $\;f\left(g(u)\right) = u, \; u \in D_g.$


13.2 Invertible functions

Moreover, $g$ is one-to-one. So we have

$g\left(f(x)\right)= x,\; x \in D_f\; $ and $\;f\left(g(u)\right) = u, \; u \in D_g.$

We say that $g$ is the inverse of $f$ and write $g = f^{-1}.$ The relation between $f$ and $g$ is symmetric; that is, $f$ is also the inverse of $g,$ and we write $$f= g^{-1}.$$


Example 13.2.1.

Let $f:\R^2\to \R^2$ be the function defined by \[ f(x, y) := \left(x-y, x+y\right). \]

Note that $f$ maps $(x,y)$ to $(u,v)$, where \begin{eqnarray*} u &=& x-y, \\ v &=& x+y. \end{eqnarray*}

Furthermore, observe that $R(f)= \R^2$ and $f$ is one-to-one, since for each $(u,v)\in \R^2 $ there is exactly one $(x,y)$ such that $f(x,y)= (u,v)$. This is so because the above system can be solved uniquely for $(x,y)$ in terms of $(u,v).$


Example 13.2.2.

Let $g:\R^2\to \R^2$ be the function defined by \[ g(x, y) := \left(x+y, 2x+2y\right). \]

Here $g$ maps $(x,y)$ to $(u,v)$ where $ \; \left\{\begin{eqnarray*} u &=& x+y, \\ v &=& 2x+2y. \end{eqnarray*} \right. $

In this case $f$ is not one-to-one, since every point on the line \[ x+ y = c, \quad (c \text{ constant}) \] is mapped onto the single point $(c,2c)$. Hence, $f$ does not have an inverse.


13.1 Inverse function theorem

Theorem: Let $U \subset \R^n$ be an open set and let $f \colon U \to \R^n$ be a continuously differentiable function. Suppose $p \in U$ and $f'(p)$ is invertible (that is, $J_f(p) \not=0$). Then there exist open sets $V, W \subset \R^n$ such that $p \in V \subset U$, $f(V) = W$ and $f|_V$ is one-to-one. Hence a function $g \colon W \to V$ exists such that $g(y) := (f|_V)^{-1}(y)$. Furthermore, $g$ is continuously differentiable and \begin{equation*} g'(y) = {\bigl(f'(x)\bigr)}^{-1}, \; \text{for all } x \in V, y = f(x). \end{equation*}
