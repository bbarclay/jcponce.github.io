39 Divergence of a vector field (div)

By the end of this section, you should be able to answer the following questions:

  • How do you calculate the divergence of a given vector field?
  • What is the significance of divergence?
  • How does it relate to flux?

39.1 Calculating divergence

Let \[ \v\left(x,y,z\right)= v_1\left(x,y,z\right)\i+ v_2\left(x,y,z\right)\j+v_3\left(x,y,z\right)\k \] be a differentiable vector function. Then the function \[ \div \v = \frac{\partial v_1}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial v_2}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial v_2}{\partial z}= \nabla \cdot \v \] is called the divergence of $\v$. Note that $\div\v$ is a scalar quantity.

Divergence has an analogous definition in two dimensions. For \[ \F\left(x,y\right) = F_1\left(x,y,z\right)\i+ F_2\left(x,y,z\right)\j \Rightarrow \div F = F_1\left(x,y,z\right)\i+ F_2\left(x,y,z\right)\j. \]

39.1.1 Example: $\v = xy^2~\i + xyz~\j + yz^2~\k$. Find $div \v$.

39.2 Understanding div in two dimensions.

Consider the flow of a two dimensional fluid with continuous velocity field $\v(x,y) = v_1(x, y)~\i + v_2(x, y)~\j$. Our aim is to calculate the outward flux from a small rectangle in the plane of area $\Delta x\Delta y$ as in the diagram below.

39.2 Understanding div in two dimensions.

We first approximate the flux across each of the four sides of the rectangle. In each case the approximation will be $\v\cdot \n ~\Delta S$, where we assume $\v$ is constant over each edge. Also let $x^*\in [x, x + \Delta x]$ and $y^*\in [y, y + \Delta y]$ represent chosen points in each interval.

Edge 1: we evaluate $\v$ at $(x^*,y)$ and assume it is constant across the entire edge. An outwardly pointing unit normal vector is $-\j$. \[ \text{flux} \approx \v\left(x^*,y\right)\cdot (-\j)~\Delta x \]

Edge 1: we evaluate $\v$ at $(x^*,y)$ and assume it is constant across the entire edge. An outwardly pointing unit normal vector is $-\j$. \[ \text{flux} \approx \v\left(x^*,y\right)\cdot (-\j)~\Delta x. \]

Edge 2: we evaluate $\v$ at $(x+\Delta x,y^*)$ and assume it is constant across the entire edge. An outwardly pointing unit normal vector is $\i$. \[ \text{flux} \approx \v\left(x+\Delta x,y^*\right)\cdot \left(~\i~\right)\Delta y. \]

Edge 3: we evaluate $\v$ at $(x^*,y+ \Delta y)$ and assume it is constant across the entire edge. An outwardly pointing unit normal vector is $\j$. \[ \text{flux} \approx \v\left(x^*,y+ \Delta y\right)\cdot \left(~\j~\right)\Delta x. \]

Edge 4: we evaluate $\v$ at $(x,y^*)$ and assume it is constant across the entire edge. An outwardly pointing unit normal vector is $-\i$. \[ \text{flux} \approx \v\left(x,y^*\right)\cdot (-\i)~\Delta y. \]

Combining all four terms gives an approximation to the net outward flux:

net outward flux

$\displaystyle \text{} $ $\displaystyle \approx \left( \v\left( x+\Delta x, y^* \right) - \v\left( x, y^* \right) \right) \cdot \i ~\Delta y \left( \v\left( x^* , y + \Delta y \right) - \v\left( x^*, y \right) \right) \cdot \j ~\Delta x $
$\displaystyle = \left( \frac{\v\left( x+\Delta x, y^* \right) - \v\left( x, y^* \right)}{\Delta x} \right) \cdot \i ~ \Delta x~\Delta y + \left( \frac{ \v\left( x^* , y + \Delta y \right) - \v\left( x^*, y \right)}{\Delta y} \right) \cdot \j ~ \Delta x~\Delta y$
$\displaystyle = \left( \frac{v_1\left( x+\Delta x, y^* \right) - v_1\left( x, y^* \right)}{\Delta x} + \frac{ v_2\left( x^* , y + \Delta y \right) - v_2\left( x^*, y \right)}{\Delta y} \right) \Delta x~\Delta y$
$\displaystyle \approx \left( \frac{\partial v_1}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v_2}{\partial y} \right) \Delta x~\Delta y$
$\displaystyle = \div(\v)~ \Delta x~\Delta y$.
