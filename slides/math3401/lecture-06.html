Implicit form of Möbius transformations (MT)

\(T(z) = w = \dfrac{az+b}{cz+d}\; (*)\), with \(ad-bc \neq 0\), can be written as \[ A z w + B z + C w + D = 0 \] with \(A=c, B=-a, C=d\) and \(D=b\); called the implicit form.

Implicit form of MT

Case I: \(c=0\). \(T\) is a bijection (1-1 and onto): \(\C\rightarrow \C\).

Case II: \((*)\) \(\implies z=\dfrac{-dw+b}{cw-a}\). Thus \[ T^{-1}(w)= \dfrac{-dw+b}{cw-a}. \]

Then in Case II, \(T\) is 1-1 and onto \(\C \setminus \left\{-d/c\right\} \rightarrow \C \setminus \left\{a/c\right\}\).

Question: Can we extend \(T\) to a function \(\C\rightarrow \C\) in Case II? In particular, so extension is 1-1 and onto.

Ans: Yes! Take \(T\left(-d/c\right)=a/c\)

Basic idea with new mappings

For \(w=1/z = \conj z /|z|^2\), we have

\(x+i\,y \mapsto u+i\,v\) and \( w = \dfrac{x-i\,y}{x^2+y^2} \), so \[ u = \dfrac{x}{x^2+y^2} \; (1)\quad \text{ & } \quad v = \dfrac{-y}{x^2+y^2} \; (2) \]

We can use this to show that \(1/z\) maps circles and lines in the \(z\)-plane to circles and lines in the \(w\)-plane.

A note on mapping circles and lines

Circles and lines in the \(z\)-plane can be represented as \[ A(x^2+y^2)+Bx+Cy +D = 0 \;\;(*) \]

with \(A,B, C, D\in R\), and \(B^2+C^2>4AD\).

If \(A = 0\) then we have a line. If \(A \neq 0\) then we have a circle.

A note on mapping circles and lines

From \((*)\) we have \[ \left( x+\frac{B}{2A} \right)^2 + \left( y+\frac{C}{2A} \right)^2 = \left( \frac{\sqrt{B^2+C^2-4AD}}{2A} \right)^2 . \]

Note that for \(w=1/z\), expressions (1) & (2) imply \[ D(u^2+v^2)+Bu-Cv+A=0. \]

Terminology reminder

\(*\; f: \Omega \rightarrow \C\) is 1-1 or injective say \[f(z) = f(\xi)\implies z=\xi.\]

\(*\; f: \Omega \rightarrow \Lambda\subseteq \C\) is onto or surjective says given \(\eta \in \Lambda\) exists (at least one) \(z\in \Omega: f(z)=\eta\).

Möbius transformation

Definition: Let \(a,b,c,d\in \C\) with \(ad-bc\neq 0\). Then \[ w = T(z) = \frac{az+b}{bz+c} \] is a Möbius (or linear fractional) transformation.

Natural domain of definition:

\(*\; c=0\): dom\((w)=\C\) (note \(c\neq 0 \implies d\neq 0\));

\(*\; c\neq 0\): dom\((w)=\C\setminus \{-d/c\}\).

Möbius transformations

Goal: Understand \(T\) geometrically

Case I: \(c=0\).

Claim: \(T\) maps \(\C \rightarrow \C\) 1-1 & onto.

Proof. (i) 1-1: Suppose \(T(z)= T(\xi)\;\; (**).\;\) We want to show: \(z=\xi\). From \((**)\) we have \[ \frac{a}{d}z+\frac{b}{d} = \frac{a}{d}\xi +\frac{b}{d} \implies z=\xi. \]

(ii) onto: given \(w\in \C\) we need \(\C\) such that \(T(z)=w\).

Check \(z=\frac{d}{a}\left(w-\frac{b}{d}\right)\) works.

Möbius transformations

Goal: Understand \(T\) geometrically

Case II: \(c\neq 0\).

\(w = \dfrac{az+b}{cz+d} =\) \( \dfrac{a\left(z+\dfrac{d}{c}\right)- \dfrac{ad}{c}+b}{c\left(z+\dfrac{d}{c}\right)}\) \(=\dfrac{a}{c}+\left(\dfrac{bc-ad}{c}\right)\cdot \dfrac{1}{cz+d}\)

In this case, \(T\) is the composition of the mappings \[ Z= cz+d; W = \frac{1}{Z}; \;\text{ & } \; w = \frac{a}{c} + \frac{bc-ad}{c}W. \]

So in both cases I & II, \(T\) is a composition of maps previously studied.
