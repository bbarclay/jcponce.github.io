Differentiability

Example 1: Find the derivative of \(f(z)=4z^2\) from first principles, i. e. using the definition.

Put \(w = f(z)\) and take \(z_0\in \C\), then

\(\displaystyle \lim_{\Delta z\ra 0} \frac{\Delta w}{\Delta z} = \lim_{\Delta z\ra 0} \frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)- f(z_0)}{\Delta z}\) \(=\displaystyle \lim_{\Delta z\ra 0} \frac{4(z_0+\Delta z )^2-4z^2_0}{\Delta z}\)

\(=\displaystyle \lim_{\Delta z\ra 0} \frac{4z_0^2+8z_0\Delta z + 4(\Delta z)^2- 4z_0^2}{\Delta z}\) \(=\displaystyle \lim_{\Delta z\ra 0} 8z_0 + 4 \Delta z \) \(= 8 z_0\).

Thus \(f'(z)= 8z\).

Differentiability

Example 2: Find the derivative of \(f(z)=|z|^2\).

\(f'\) does not exists, except at \(z=0\).

Note: differentiability \(\Rightarrow\) continuity

continuity \(\nRightarrow\) differentiability

Example for \(\nRightarrow\): \(|z|^2\) (or \(|z|\), or others).

Formulae

(cf. \(f:\R \ra \R\))

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dz}(c) = 0\); \(c\) constant
  • \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dz}z^n = nz^{n-1}\); \(n\in\Z\)
  • \(\displaystyle e^z = e^z\)
  • \(\displaystyle \sin z = \cos z\)
  • \(\displaystyle \cos z = -sin z\)

Formulae

For \(f, g\) differentiable functions:

  • \(\displaystyle \big(f\pm g\big)^{\prime} = f^{\prime} \pm g^{\prime}\)
  • \(\displaystyle \big(f g\big)^{\prime} = fg^{\prime} + f^{\prime}g\)
  • \(\displaystyle \left(\frac{f}{g}\right)^{\prime} = \frac{gf^{\prime} - f g^{\prime}}{g^2}\)
  • Chain rule: \(f\) differentiable at \(z_0\) and \(g\) differentiable at \(f(z_0)\), then \(g\circ f\) is differentiable at \(z_0\) and \(\big(g \circ f\big) = g^{\prime}\big(f(z_0)\big)f^{\prime}(z_0)\)

Cauchy-Riemann

\(f : z \mapsto w = u(x,y) + iv(x,y)\) . Suppose \(f\) is differentiable at \(z_0 = x_0+iy_0\). Set \(\Delta z = \Delta x + i \Delta y \) ... (♣️) \[f^{\prime} (z_0) = \lim_{z\ra z_0}\frac{ \Delta w }{ \Delta z} \quad (1)\]

Key point: Derivative is independent of how \[\Delta z \ra 0 \quad (*)\]

Cauchy-Riemann

Note \(\Delta w = f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0) \)

\(= u(x_0 +\Delta x, y_0 +\Delta y) + i v(x_0 +\Delta x, y_0 +\Delta y) \quad (2) \)

Now, from (♣️) we have

\(f^{\prime}(z_0) = \displaystyle \lim_{\left(\Delta x, \Delta y\right)\ra (0,0)} \Re \left(\frac{\Delta w}{\Delta z}\right) + i \lim_{\left(\Delta x, \Delta y\right)\ra (0,0)} \Im \left(\frac{\Delta w}{\Delta z}\right) \quad (3) \)

As per \((*)\), the value of the limit is independent of how \(\left(\Delta x, \Delta y\right)\ra (0,0)\).

Cauchy-Riemann

To start, let \(\left(\Delta x, \Delta y\right)\ra (0,0)\) along the \(x\)-axis, i. e. consider \(\Delta z\) of the form \(\left(\Delta x, 0\right)\) with \(\Delta x\neq 0\). So \[ \frac{\Delta w}{\Delta z} = \frac{u(x_0 +\Delta x, y_0) - u(x_0,y_0)}{\Delta x} + i \frac{v(x_0 +\Delta x, y_0) - v(x_0,y_0)}{\Delta x} \]

Hence

\(\displaystyle \lim_{\left(\Delta x, \Delta y\right)\ra (0,0)} \Re \left(\frac{\Delta w}{\Delta z}\right) \) \(= u_x(x_0,y_0)\) \(= \displaystyle \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(x_0,y_0) \quad (4)\)

\(\displaystyle \lim_{\left(\Delta x, \Delta y\right)\ra (0,0)} \Im \left(\frac{\Delta w}{\Delta z}\right) \) \(= v_x(x_0,y_0)\) \(= \displaystyle \frac{\partial v}{\partial x}(x_0,y_0)\quad (5)\)

Cauchy-Riemann

Repeat for \(\Delta z\) of the form \(\Delta z = 0 + i \Delta y\) with \(\Delta y\neq 0\), i. e. \(\Delta z \ra 0\) on the \(y\)-axis. For this \(\Delta z\): \[ \frac{\Delta w}{\Delta z} = \frac{u(x_0, y_0+\Delta y) - u(x_0,y_0)}{i \Delta y} + i \frac{v(x_0, y_0+\Delta y) - v(x_0,y_0)}{i \Delta y} \]

So

\(\displaystyle \lim_{\left(\Delta x, \Delta y\right)\ra (0,0)} \Re \left(\frac{\Delta w}{\Delta z}\right) \) \(= v_y(x_0,y_0) \quad (6)\)

\(\displaystyle \lim_{\left(\Delta x, \Delta y\right)\ra (0,0)} \Im \left(\frac{\Delta w}{\Delta z}\right) \) \(= -u_y(x_0,y_0) \quad (7)\)

Cauchy-Riemann

Keeping in mind \((*)\), ((4) & (6)) and ((5) & (7)) imply the Cauchy-Riemann equations:

If \(f=u+iv\) is differentiable at \(z_0=x_0+iy_0\), then \[ \begin{align} u_x & = v_y \\ -v_x & = u_y \end{align} \] at \((x_0, y_0)\).

Necessary & sufficient conditions

Note: We have shown that Cauchy-Riemann (C/R) are necessary for complex differentiability. They are not sufficient (later).

Sufficient conditions for \(f^{\prime}(z_0)\) to exist:

  • \(f\) is defined in a neibourhood of \(z_0\),
  • \(u_x, u_y, v_x, v_y\) are defined and continuous in a beighbourhood at \((x_0,y_0)\),
  • C/R hold at \((x_0,y_0)\).


Then \(f^{\prime}(z_0)\) exists.
