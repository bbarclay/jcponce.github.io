The logarithm and the exponential

We now have the tools needed to properly define the exponential and the logarithm that you know so well. We start with exponentiation.

If $n$ is a positive integer, it is obvious to define \begin{equation*} x^n := \underbrace{x \cdot x \cdot \cdots \cdot x}_{n \text{ times}} . \end{equation*}

$x^0:=1$. For negative integers, let $x^{-n} := \frac{1}{x^n}$. If $x > 0$, define $x^{1/n}$ as the unique positive $n$th root. Finally, for a rational number $\frac{n}{m}$ (in lowest terms), define \begin{equation*} x^{n/m} := {\bigl(x^{1/m}\bigr)}^n . \end{equation*}

The logarithm and the exponential

Now, what do we mean by $\sqrt{3}^{\sqrt{5}}$? Or $x^y$ in general? In particular, what is $e^x$ for all $x$? And how do we solve $y=e^x$ for $x$?

Here we are going to answer these questions and more.

The logarithm

Proposition 5.4.1: There exists a unique function $L \colon (0,\infty) \to \R$ such that

  1. $L(1) = 0$.
  2. $L$ is differentiable and $L'(x) = \frac{1}{x}$.
  3. $L$ is strictly increasing, bijective, and \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 0} L(x) = -\infty , \qquad \text{and} \qquad \lim_{x\to \infty} L(x) = \infty . \end{equation*}
  4. $L(xy) = L(x)+L(y)$ for all $x,y \in (0,\infty)$.
  5. If $q$ is a rational number and $x > 0$, then $L(x^q) = q L(x)$.

The logarithm

Proof: To show the existence, we define a good candidate and show it satisfies all the properties. For example, let \begin{equation*} L(x) := \int_1^x \frac{1}{t}~dt . \end{equation*} With this we can easily see that (i) holds since $L(1)=0$.

Now, notice that property (ii) holds by the second form of the fundamental theorem of calculus.

The logarithm

Proof (cont): To prove property (iv), we just apply a change of variables $u=yt$ to obtain

$L(x) = \displaystyle \int_1^{x} \frac{1}{t}~dt$ $= \displaystyle \int_y^{xy} \frac{1}{u}~du $ $= \displaystyle \int_1^{xy} \frac{1}{u}~du - \int_1^{y} \frac{1}{u}~du $ $\displaystyle= L(xy)-L(y) .$




The logarithm

Proof (cont): Now to prove property (iii), we use (ii) and the fact that $L'(x) = \frac{1}{x} > 0$ for $x > 0$, implies that $L$ is strictly increasing and hence one-to-one.
Let us show $L$ is onto. As $\frac{1}{t} \geq \frac{1}{2}$ when $t \in [1,2]$, \begin{equation*} L(2) = \int_1^2 \frac{1}{t} ~dt \geq \frac{1}{2} . \end{equation*} By induction, (iii) implies that for $n \in \N$ \begin{equation*} L(2^n) = L(2) + L(2) + \cdots + L(2) = n L(2) . \end{equation*}

The logarithm

Proof (cont): Given $y > 0$, by the Archimedean property of the real numbers (notice $L(2) > 0$), there is an $n \in \N$ such that $L(2^n) > y$.

 By the intermediate value theorem there is an $x_1 \in (1,2^n)$ such that $L(x_1) = y$. Then we get $(0,\infty)$ is in the image of $L$.

 As $L$ is increasing, $L(x) > y$ for all $x > 2^n$, and so $ \lim_{x\to\infty} L(x) = \infty . $

 Next $0 = L\left(\frac{x}{x}\right) = L(x) + L\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)$, and so $L(x) = - L\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)$. Using $x=2^{-n}$, we obtain as above that $L$ achieves all negative numbers. This implies that

$\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad \displaystyle \lim_{x \to 0} L(x) = \lim_{x \to 0} -L\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) $ $= \displaystyle \lim_{x \to \infty} -L(x) = - \infty .$

 In the limits, note that only negative values ($x > 0$) are in the domain of $L$.

The logarithm

Proof (cont): Item (v) is left as an exercise.

 Finally we will prove that is unique using properties (i) and (ii). Via the first form of the fundamental theorem of calculus, \begin{equation*} L(x) = \int_1^x \frac{1}{t}~dt \end{equation*} is the unique function such that $L(1) = 0$ and $L'(x) = \frac{1}{x}$. $\blacksquare$

Now that we proved that there is a unique function with these properties, we simply define the logarithm or sometimes called the natural logarithm: \begin{equation*} \ln(x) := L(x) . \end{equation*}

The exponential

Proposition 5.4.2: There exists a unique function $E \colon \R \to (0,\infty)$ such that

  1. $E(0) = 1$.
  2. $E$ is differentiable and $E'(x) = E(x)$.
  3. $E$ is strictly increasing, bijective, and \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to -\infty} E(x) = 0 , \qquad \text{and} \qquad \lim_{x\to \infty} E(x) = \infty . \end{equation*}
  4. $E(x+y) = E(x)E(y)$ for all $x,y \in \R$.
  5. If $q$ is a rational number, then $E(qx) = E(x)^q$.
