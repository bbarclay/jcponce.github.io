Problem 1

Let \(F\) be an ordered field and $x,y,z \in F$. Prove that if \(x < 0\) and \(y < z\), then \(xy> xz\).

Proof:
If $x\lt 0$ and $y\lt z$, then $-x\gt 0$ and $0\lt z-y$. Here I am using
Prop. 1.1.8 i), Def. 1.1.5 (A5), Def. 1.1.7 i)

Then $0\lt -x(z-y)$, by Def. 1.1.7 ii).

Implies $0\lt -xz+xy$, by Def. 1.1.5 (D).

Then $xz\lt xy$, using Def. 1.1.5 (A5). $\blacksquare$

Problem 3

Let $S$ be an ordered set. Let $B \subset S$ be bounded (above and below). Let $A \subset B$ be a nonempty subset. Suppose all the $\inf$s and $\sup$s exist. Show that \begin{equation*} \inf\, B \leq \inf\, A \leq \sup\, A \leq \sup\, B . \end{equation*}

Proof:
Notice that $\inf A\leq \sup A$, since $A\subset B\subset S$, $B$ is bounded and $S$ is ordered. (Def. 1.1.3)

I just need to prove that $\inf B\leq \inf A$ and $\sup A\leq \sup B$.

Problem 3

Proof (cont.):
To prove $\inf B\leq \inf A$, let $x\in A$. This implies $x\in B$ because $A\subset B$.

Then $\inf B\leq x$ by Def. 1.1.3. So $\inf B$ is a lower bound of $A$, since $x$ was taken arbitrary in $A$.

Since $\inf A$ is the greatest lower bound, then $\inf B\leq \inf A$.





Problem 3

Proof (cont.):
Finally, to prove that $\sup A\leq \sup B$, let $x\in A$. Again this implies $x\in B$ because $A\subset B$.

Then $x\leq \sup B$ by Def. 1.1.3. So $\sup B$ is an upper bound of $A$, since $x$ was taken arbitrary in $A$.

Since $\sup A$ is the lowest upper bound, then $\sup A\leq \sup B$. $\blacksquare$
