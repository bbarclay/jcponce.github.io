Roots of a complex number

\(n\) roots of \(z=e^{i\theta}\), \(n>1\) means: We want all \(w\in \C\) such that \(w^n=z\).

Notation: \(\exp(\xi)= e^{\xi}\).

Roots of a complex number

\(z\) has \(n\) distinct \(n\)th roots:

\[{\small \begin{align*} & \left\{ z^{1/n}\exp\left(\frac{i\theta}{n}\right), z^{1/n}\exp\left(\frac{i\theta}{n} + \frac{i2\pi}{n}\right), \right. \\ & \quad \left. z^{1/n}\exp\left(\frac{i\theta}{n} + \frac{i4\pi}{n}\right), \ldots, z^{1/n}\exp\left(\frac{i\theta}{n} + \frac{i2(n-1)\pi}{n} \right) \right\} \end{align*}} \]

Functions and mappings

\(\Omega \subseteq \C\): A function \(f:\Omega \rightarrow \C\) can be viewed as a mapping on \(\Omega\), the domain of \(f\).

If \(\Omega \) is not speficified, take \(\Omega \) as large as possible.

For example for \(f(z)=\frac{1}{z}\), the domain is \(\C_*\) and \(f:\C_*\rightarrow \C\).

We can write: \(f:z \mapsto \frac{1}{z}\) or \(w=\frac{1}{z}\); or just \(1/z\).

Complex conjugate

Properties

(v) \( |z^2| = z \cdot \conj{z}, \; z\neq 0 \).

(vi) \( \Re(z) = \frac{z+\conj{z}}{2}\), \(\Im(z) = \frac{z-\conj{z}}{2i}\)

(vii) \(\conj{z + w} = \conj{z} +\conj{w} \).

Triangle inequality

\(|z+w|\leq |z| + |w|\)

Proof:

\(|z+w|^2=|z|^2+|w|^2-2|z||w|\cos A\)

\(|z+w|^2\leq |z|^2+|w|^2-2|z||w|\),
since \(\cos A \geq -1\).

Then \(|z+w|^2 \leq \left(|z|^2+|w|^2\right)^2\).

Take \(\sqrt{}\) in both sides and we are done \(\blacksquare\).

Polar coordinates

\( \left\{ \begin{array}{r} x = r\cos \theta \\ y = r\cos \theta \end{array} \right. \)

\[ \begin{align*} z &= r e^{i \theta} \\ &= r \left(\cos \theta + i \, \sin \theta \right)\\ &= r \text{cis}\theta \end{align*} \]

Argument

\(r e^{i \theta}\) formally follows from Taylor series of \(e^{i\theta}\).

Here \(\theta\) is an argument of \(z\) and we write \(\theta = \arg(z)\).

The \(\arg \) is not a (single-valued) function.

If \(\theta\) is an argument of \(z\), \(\Arg(z)\) is defined to be the unique value of \(\theta\) with \[-\pi < \arg (z) \leq \pi\]

Argument

\(\Arg(1+i)=\frac{\pi}{4}\), but \[\arg(1+i) = \ldots\, -\frac{7\pi}{4}, \frac{\pi}{4}, \frac{9\pi}{4}, \ldots.\]

\(\Arg(-6)=\pi\).

\(\Arg(0)\) is undefined.

\(\arg(0)=\R\).

Argument

So \(\Arg\) is a function: \[\C\setminus \{0\} \rightarrow \R \]

\(\C\setminus \{0\} = \C^* = \C_*\).

Argument

Notice also that \(|e^{i\theta}|=1\).

\(\left(e^{i\theta} \right)^{-1} = e^{-i\theta}= \conj{e^{i\theta}}\).

\(\left( r e^{i\theta} \right) \left( \rho e^{i\phi} \right) = r \rho e^{i\left(\theta + \phi\right)} \).

So \( |z\, w| = |z|\,|w|\) and \[\arg(z\, w) = \arg(z) + \arg(w).\]

Argument

But \( \Arg(z\, w)\) may fail to be equal to \[\Arg(z)+\Arg(w).\]

E. g. \( z = w = \frac{-1+i}{2}\).

\( \Arg(z) = \Arg(w) = \frac{3\pi}{4}\).

\( z\,w = -i \implies \Arg(z \, w) = -\frac{\pi}{2}\).

but \( \Arg(z)+\Arg(w) = \frac{3\pi}{2}\).

de Moivre's formula

Let \(z = r e^{i\theta}\), then \(z^n = r^n e^{i n \theta}\), \(n\in \N\).

For \(r=1\), \[ \begin{align*} e^{i n \theta} &= \left(\cos \theta + \sin \theta \right)^n \\ &= \cos \left(n\theta\right) + i\,\sin \left(n\theta\right) \end{align*} \]

Example: \[ \begin{align*} \left(1+i\right)^7 &= \left( \sqrt{2} e^{i\pi/4} \right)^7 \\ &= \left(\sqrt{2}\right)^7 e^{i7\pi/4} = 8-8i. \end{align*} \]
