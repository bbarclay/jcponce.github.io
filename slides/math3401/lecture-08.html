Inverse of \(\exp\)

Too much to ask to hope for "\(=\log = \log_e\)", due to \(\exp\) being periodic (period \(2\pi i\)) in \(\C\).

Inverse of \(\exp\)

Note that \(e^w=z\). Write \(z = r e^{i \Theta}\), with \(r> 0\) and \(\Theta = \Arg(z) \in (-\pi, \pi]\).

Write \(w= u+iv\). Then \(z = e^{w} = e^{u+iv} = e^u e^{iv} \).

This implies \[ \left\{ \begin{align*} e^u &= r \\ v &= \Theta + 2 k \pi ,\; k\in \Z \end{align*} \right. \]

Inverse of \(\exp\)

This defines the multi-valued function \(\log\) on \(\C_*\).

Note: \(\exp\left(\log z\right) = z\) and \(\log\left(\exp z\right)= z+ 2 k \pi\)

Inverse of \(\exp\)

As with \(\Arg\)/\(\arg\), define \[\Log (z) = \ln |z| + i \Arg(z) \text{ on } C_*.\]

\(\Log\) is a single value, but discontinuous on the negative real axis, including \({0}\), since \(\Arg\) is.

Indeed, \(\Arg\)/\(\Log\) are not even defined at \(0\).

\(\Log\) is called the principal logarithm.

As with \(\Arg\), there may hold: \[ \Log\left(z_1 z_2\right)\neq \Log z_1 + \Log z_2. \]

Complex exponent

Take \(c\in \C\). Set \[ z^c \exp \left( c \log z\right), \quad z\neq 0. \]

Note: corresponds to usual index laws for \(c=n\in \Z\) and for \(c=1/n\), we recover what we did in Lecture 4.

Remark: Brown & Churchill define \(z^{1/n}\) as a multi-valued function, and in particular defines the Principal Value, that is \[ \text{PV}\left(z^{1/n}\right) = |z|^{1/n}\exp \left(\frac{i \Arg(z)}{n}\right). \]

Complex exponent

Same procedure works for \(z\mapsto z^c\): \[ \begin{align*} \text{PV}\left(z^{c}\right) &= \exp \big(c\,\Log (z)\big) \\ &= \exp\big(c \ln |z| + i \Arg (z)\big). \end{align*} \]

E. g. \(\text{PV}\left[(1+i)^{4i}\right]\):

\(\qquad\qquad =\exp\big( 4i \left[ \ln |1-i| + i \Arg (1-i) \right] \big)\)

\(\qquad\qquad =\exp\big( 4i\ln\sqrt{2} -4(\pi/4) \big)\)

\(\qquad\qquad =e^{\pi} \exp \left(4i\ln \sqrt{2}\right)\)

\(\qquad\qquad =e^{\pi} \cos \left(2\ln 2\right) + i e^{\pi} \sin \left(2\ln 2\right)\).

Remark

Sometimes need to use a different single-valued \(\log \) and \(\arg\), e. g. may nee to integrate around a contour.

In this case, choose/define \(\arg(z)\) such that \[ \frac{\pi}{2}< \arg (z) \leq \frac{3\pi}{2} \] where this \(\arg(z)\) is a particlar value of \(\arg(z)\), which leads to a new, single-valued \(\Log\), etc.
