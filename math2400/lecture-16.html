The logarithm and the exponential

We now have the tools to properly define the exponential and the logarithm that you know so well. We start with exponentiation.

If $n$ is a positive integer, we define \begin{equation*} x^n := \underbrace{x \cdot x \cdot \cdots \cdot x}_{n \text{ times}} . \end{equation*}




The logarithm and the exponential

We also define $x^0:=1$. For negative integers, $$x^{-n} := \dfrac{1}{x^n}.$$ If $x > 0$, define $x^{1/n}$ as the unique positive $n$th root.

Finally, for a rational number $\dfrac{n}{m}$ (in lowest terms), define \begin{equation*} x^{n/m} := {\bigl(x^{1/m}\bigr)}^n . \end{equation*}


The logarithm and the exponential

Now, what do we mean by $\sqrt{3}^{\sqrt{5}}$? Or $x^y$ in general? In particular, what is $e^x$ for all $x$? And how do we solve $y=e^x$ for $x$?

Here we are going to answer these questions.






8.3 The logarithm

Theorem 8.3.1. There exists a unique function $L \colon (0,\infty) \to \R$ such that

  1. $L(1) = 0$.
  2. $L$ is differentiable and $L'(x) = \dfrac{1}{x}$.
  3. $L$ is strictly increasing, bijective, and \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to 0} L(x) = -\infty , \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{x\to \infty} L(x) = \infty . \end{equation*}
  4. $L(xy) = L(x)+L(y)$ for all $x,y \in (0,\infty)$.
  5. If $q$ is a rational number and $x > 0$, then $L(x^q) = q L(x)$.


8.3 The logarithm

Proof. To show the existence, we define a good candidate and show it satisfies all the properties. For example, let \begin{equation*} L(x) := \int_1^x \frac{1}{t}~dt . \end{equation*} With this we can easily see that (1) holds since $L(1)=0$.

Now, notice that property (2) holds by the second form of the fundamental theorem of calculus.

Proof. To prove property (4), we just apply a change of variables $u=yt$ to obtain

$L(x) = \displaystyle \int_1^{x} \frac{1}{t}~dt$ $= \displaystyle \int_y^{xy} \frac{1}{u}~du $

$\qquad = \displaystyle \int_1^{xy} \frac{1}{u}~du - \int_1^{y} \frac{1}{u}~du $ $\displaystyle= L(xy)-L(y) .$

Proof. Now to prove property (3), we use (2) and the fact that $L'(x) = \frac{1}{x} > 0$ for $x > 0$, implies that $L$ is strictly increasing and hence one-to-one. Let us show $L$ is onto. As $\frac{1}{t} \geq \frac{1}{2}$ when $t \in [1,2]$,

$\ds L(2) = \int_1^2 \frac{1}{t} ~dt $ $\ds \geq \frac{1}{2} .$

By induction, (3) implies that for $n \in \N$

$ L(2^n) $ $ = \underbrace{L(2) + L(2) + \cdots + L(2)}_{n-\text{times}} $ $= n L(2) . $

Proof. Given $y > 0$, by the Archimedean property of the real numbers (notice $L(2) > 0$), there is an $n \in \N$ such that $L(2^n) > y$.

 By the intermediate value theorem there is an $x_1 \in (1,2^n)$ such that $L(x_1) = y$. Then we get $(0,\infty)$ is in the image of $L$.

 As $L$ is increasing, $L(x) > y$ for all $x > 2^n$, and so $ \lim_{x\to\infty} L(x) = \infty . $

Now that we proved that there is a unique function with these properties, we simply define the logarithm or sometimes called the natural logarithm: \begin{equation*} \ln(x) := L(x) . \end{equation*}






8.4 The exponential

Proposition 8.4.1. There exists a unique function $E \colon \R \to (0,\infty)$ such that

  1. $E(0) = 1$.
  2. $E$ is differentiable and $E'(x) = E(x)$.
  3. $E$ is strictly increasing, bijective, and \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to -\infty} E(x) = 0 , \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{x\to \infty} E(x) = \infty . \end{equation*}
  4. $E(x+y) = E(x)E(y)$ for all $x,y \in \R$.
  5. If $q$ is a rational number, then $E(qx) = E(x)^q$.


8.4 The exponential

Proof. As we did before for $\ln x$, we prove existence of such a function by defining a candidate and proving that it satisfies all the properties.

The $L(x) = \ln (x)$ defined previously is invertible. Let $E$ be the inverse function of $L$. Property (1) is immediate.

Property (2) follows via the inverse function theorem. The function $L$ satisfies all the hypotheses of this theorem, and hence

$ E'(x) = \dfrac{1}{L'\bigl(E(x)\bigr)} = E(x) . $


8.4 The exponential

Proof. Now let us prove property (3). The function $E$ is strictly increasing since $E'(x) = E(x) > 0$. As $E$ is the inverse of $L$, it must also be bijective. To find the limits, we use that $E$ is strictly increasing and onto $(0,\infty)$. For every $M > 0$, there is an $x_0$ such that $E(x_0) = M$ and $E(x) \geq M$ for all $x \geq x_0$.

Similarly, for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there is an $x_0$ such that $E(x_0) = \varepsilon$ and $E(x) \lt \varepsilon$ for all $x \lt x_0$. Therefore, \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\to -\infty} E(x) = 0 , \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{x\to \infty} E(x) = \infty . \end{equation*}


8.4 The exponential

Proof. To prove property (iv), we use the corresponding property for the logarithm. Take $x, y \in \R$. As $L$ is bijective, find $a$ and $b$ such that $x = L(a)$ and $y = L(b)$. Then

$ E(x+y) = E\bigl(L(a)+L(b)\bigr) $ $ = E\bigl(L(ab)\bigr) $ $ = ab $ $ = E(x)E(y) . $

Property (5) is left as an exercise.




8.4 The exponential

Proof. Uniqueness follows from (1) and (2). Let $E$ and $F$ be two functions satisfying (1) and (2).

 $ \quad\displaystyle\frac{d}{dx} \Bigl( F(x)E(-x) \Bigr) = F'(x)E(-x) - E'(-x)F(x) $ $ \qquad\qquad\qquad\quad\;\;\, = F(x)E(-x) - E(-x)F(x) $ $ =0 . $

 Therefore, by Theorem 6.3.1, $F(x)E(-x) $ $= F(0)E(-0) $ $ = 1$ for all $x \in \R$. Next, $1 = E(0)$ $ E(x-x) $ $ =E(x) E(-x)$. Then

$ 0 = 1-1 $ $ =F(x)E(-x) - E(x)E(-x) $ $ = \bigl(F(x)-E(x)\bigr) E(-x) . $

Finally, $E(-x) \not= 0$ for all $x \in \R$. So $F(x)-E(x) = 0$ for all $x$, and this completes the proof. $\blacksquare$


8.4 The exponential

Now we can define the exponential function as \begin{equation*} \exp(x) := E(x) . \end{equation*}

If $y \in \Q$ and $x > 0$, then \begin{equation*} x^y = \exp\bigl(\ln(x^y)\bigr) = \exp\bigl(y\ln(x)\bigr) . \end{equation*}

We can now make sense of exponentiation $x^y$ for arbitrary $y \in \R$; if $x > 0$ and $y$ is irrational, define \begin{equation*} x^y := \exp\bigl(y\ln(x)\bigr) . \end{equation*}


8.4 The exponential

Define the number $e$, sometimes called Euler's number or the base of the natural logarithm, as \begin{equation*} e := \exp(1) . \end{equation*}

The notation $e^x$ for $\exp(x)$: \begin{equation*} e^x = \exp\bigl(x \ln(e) \bigr) = \exp(x) . \end{equation*}

  2. If $x > 0$, then $\ln(x^y) = y \ln (x)$.
